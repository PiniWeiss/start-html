The rise of Nazism

A quick overview of the movement that brought disaster to the world

The rise of the Nazi Party (NSDAP) from a fringe group to the ruling power in Germany is one of the most significant transformations in modern history. Its ascent was fueled by a combination of economic despair, political instability, and the charismatic, yet manipulative, leadership of Adolf Hitler. In the aftermath of World War I, Germany was reeling from a humiliating defeat and the harsh conditions of the Treaty of Versailles. The nascent Weimar Republic faced hyperinflation and social unrest, creating a vacuum for extremist ideologies. Hitler joined the German Workers' Party in 1919, quickly renaming it the National Socialist German Workers' Party and utilizing his oratory skills to capitalize on public grievances. He promoted a platform of ultra-nationalism, anti-Semitism, and the "Stab-in-the-back" myth, which blamed Jews and socialists for Germany's loss in the war. The turning point came with the Great Depression in 1929. As unemployment skyrocketed and the middle class collapsed, the Nazis' promises of "Work and Bread" resonated with a desperate electorate. Between 1928 and 1932, the party's share of the vote surged from under 3% to 37%. The Nazis also utilized sophisticated propaganda led by Joseph Goebbels and paramilitary violence via the SA (Brownshirts) to intimidate opponents. In January 1933, conservative politicians, believing they could "tame" Hitler, convinced President Hindenburg to appoint him Chancellor. Once in power, the Nazis exploited the Reichstag Fire to suspend civil liberties and passed the Enabling Act, effectively establishing a totalitarian dictatorship and ending German democracy."
